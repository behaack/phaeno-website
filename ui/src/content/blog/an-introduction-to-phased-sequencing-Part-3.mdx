## Summary

Molecule-resolved transcript data describe RNA at a finer level than gene counts alone. Instead of treating every transcript from a locus as interchangeable, they can represent isoform structures that meet predefined completeness criteria, linked sequence variants, observed transcript boundaries, predicted coding sequences, and regulatory elements as features of individual captured molecular templates.

Here, **complete** and **full-length** refer to predefined analytical criteria, not to molecular assignment alone. A study should state whether completeness requires observed 5′ and 3′ boundaries, a complete splice chain, minimum coverage, an end-to-end contiguous consensus, or reference-supported interpolation between covered regions.

That additional structure could be useful in three broad settings. Biological discovery may benefit from a clearer view of transcript diversity and regulation. Translational research may be able to connect specific molecular forms with disease mechanisms, patient subgroups, or treatment response. Machine-learning studies may gain features that preserve biological distinctions obscured by gene-level aggregation.

None of these outcomes follows automatically from higher resolution. Molecule-resolved data also introduce more features, lower counts per feature, new measurement errors, annotation challenges, and additional opportunities for overfitting. Their value must be demonstrated in direct comparisons with established measurements.

The central question is not whether molecule-level data contain more detail. It is whether that detail is accurate, reproducible, biologically relevant, and useful for a defined decision.

## From transcript molecules to analytical features

A captured RNA-derived molecular template can carry several kinds of information at once: observed 5′ and 3′ transcript boundaries, exon combinations, predicted coding sequence, untranslated regions, candidate editing events, and sequence variants. Molecule resolution preserves the relationships among those features. When assay performance and validation justify it, observed boundaries may support inference of promoter, transcription-start, or cleavage and polyadenylation usage.

Downstream analysis does not need to use every molecule as a separate variable. Molecule-level observations can be summarized into interpretable features such as:

The appropriate feature representation depends on the scientific question. A discovery study may retain a broad catalog of transcripts. A mechanistic study may focus on one splice event in a defined molecular context. A predictive model may require a compact, pre-specified feature set that can be measured consistently across cohorts.

## Biological discovery: resolving the transcript landscape

Transcript diversity is extensive and context dependent. Different tissues, cell types, developmental stages, environmental exposures, and disease states can show different observed 5′ boundaries, inferred promoter usage, splice patterns, and observed 3′ transcript boundaries.

Large long-read studies provide evidence that access to extended transcript structure changes what can be observed. Glinos and colleagues used long-read RNA sequencing across human tissues to identify extensive transcript diversity and analyze allele-associated transcript structures. A long-read atlas of 29 immune-cell subsets identified thousands of cell-type-specific isoforms and connected transcript features with genetic and disease-associated analyses.

These studies demonstrate the scientific value of linked transcript structure. They do not imply that every newly observed isoform is functional. Candidate transcripts may arise from biological noise, incomplete processing, technical artifacts, or rare events with little phenotypic consequence.

Discovery therefore requires several layers of evidence:

- reproducible observation across technical and biological replicates;
- confirmation of observed transcript boundaries and splice junctions;
- quantitative support across relevant samples or cell populations;
- orthogonal evidence from targeted assays or another measurement platform;
- perturbation or functional experiments when a causal role is proposed.

Molecule-resolved data can improve the starting map. Biological interpretation still depends on experimental design and validation.

## Translational research: connecting molecular form with phenotype

Gene-level RNA abundance can remain stable while transcript composition changes. One isoform may increase as another decreases, leaving the total gene count approximately unchanged. If the isoforms have different predicted open reading frames or regulatory regions, the biological state may change without a large gene-level signal. RNA sequence supports inference of coding potential; it does not by itself establish which open reading frame is translated or whether a protein product is stable.

That creates several translational hypotheses. Isoform features might help distinguish molecular subgroups, explain variable pathway activity, connect genetic variants with altered transcripts, or identify forms associated with disease course or treatment response.

Primary studies show why these hypotheses are plausible. Full-length isoform sequencing in cardiomyocytes identified transcript changes associated with disease-relevant RBM20 mutations. Long-read immune-cell profiling connected cell-type-specific isoforms with disease-associated genetic analyses. These results illustrate possible routes from transcript structure to mechanism.

They also illustrate the limitations. Findings can depend on tissue composition, cell state, sample quality, sequencing depth, analytical filters, and reference annotation. Associations discovered in one cohort may not reproduce in another. A marker can be biologically interesting without being accurate enough for prediction or useful enough to change a decision.

## Machine learning: a different feature space

Most transcriptomic models begin with a matrix in which rows represent samples and columns represent genes. Each value summarizes the abundance of RNA assigned to a locus. This representation is compact and often effective, but it can collapse multiple molecular forms into one number.

Molecule-resolved data make alternative representations possible. Features could describe the estimated abundance of specific isoforms, the ratio of isoforms within a gene, the presence of linked sequence events, observed transcript-end usage, or distributions of molecular structures.

Recovered molecule groups are analytical observations of captured templates, not automatically unbiased census counts of all RNA molecules in the input. Capture probability, reverse-transcription efficiency, molecular splitting and collision, identifier dropout, duplicate representation, differential recovery by length or sequence, filtering, and assembly thresholds can all affect the relationship between observed groups and input abundance.

The potential advantage is specificity. A feature tied to a defined molecular form may align more closely with a regulatory process or disease mechanism than total gene expression. Primary studies have evaluated isoform-level features in predictive models, including models of breast-cancer histological grade, demonstrating that direct comparisons with gene-level baselines are feasible.

The potential disadvantage is dimensionality. A sample may contain many rare or incompletely annotated isoforms. Splitting one gene count among many transcript features lowers the observations available for each feature. Measurement and annotation changes can create apparent features that do not reproduce.

Common risks include:

- **Overfitting:** a model learns cohort-specific isoforms or technical artifacts rather than generalizable biology.
- **Data leakage:** feature selection or normalization uses information from the evaluation set.
- **Batch confounding:** library, instrument, site, or processing differences correlate with the outcome.
- **Feature instability:** transcript definitions or abundance estimates change with software, reference annotations, or measurement depth.
- **Sparse observations:** rare isoforms produce unreliable estimates or are absent from many samples.
- **Loss of interpretability:** a large feature space obscures which molecular relationships drive the prediction.

These risks are manageable, but only when they are addressed in the study design rather than after a favorable model result appears.

## Designing decisive comparisons

The value of molecule-resolved data should be tested against the strongest established alternative, not against an intentionally weak baseline. For many questions, that means comparing models or analyses built from:

- standard gene-level RNA-sequencing features;
- established splice-junction or transcript-abundance estimates;
- relevant clinical, imaging, genomic, or proteomic variables;
- combined feature sets that test whether molecule-resolved data add independent information.

A useful study should predefine the biological question, measurement protocol, feature-generation pipeline, primary metric, comparison baseline, and validation cohort. Discovery and evaluation should be separated. Whenever possible, the final analysis should be locked before the independent cohort is examined.

## Selected scientific context

- [Transcriptome variation in human tissues revealed by long-read sequencing](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-05035-y) — primary evidence that linked transcript structure can reveal tissue diversity, allele-associated effects, and novel transcripts.
- [Single-molecule, full-length transcript isoform sequencing reveals disease-associated RNA isoforms in cardiomyocytes](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-021-24484-z) — an example of full-length isoform analysis in a disease-relevant cellular model.
- [Long-read sequencing for 29 immune cell subsets reveals disease-linked isoforms](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-024-48615-4) — an immune-cell atlas connecting isoform structure, cell specificity, and disease-associated analyses.
- [Determining breast cancer histological grade from RNA-sequencing data](https://doi.org/10.1186/s13058-016-0705-4) — an example of comparing gene- and isoform-level features in predictive modeling.

These studies establish scientific precedent for evaluating extended and isoform-level transcript information under platform-specific definitions of completeness. They do not establish the performance of PSeq or guarantee that molecule-resolved features will improve a particular discovery, translational, or machine-learning application.

## Conclusion

Molecule-resolved transcript data could expand transcriptomics from a map of active genes into a map of the molecular forms those genes produce. That may reveal linked sequence and regulatory relationships that are difficult to recover from gene-level or pooled-fragment summaries.

The potential applications are broad: transcript discovery, cell-state characterization, disease-mechanism research, molecular stratification, and predictive modeling. The evidentiary requirements are equally broad. Each application must establish measurement performance, biological relevance, reproducibility, incremental value, and—where appropriate—real-world utility.

PSeq is designed to make molecule-resolved transcript measurement compatible with established short-read sequencing systems. Whether that architecture produces useful new information will be answered by direct experiments, careful comparisons, and independent validation.
