## Summary

DNA sequence establishes the inherited framework of biological systems, but it does not by itself specify the molecular state of a cell. That state emerges through transcription and RNA processing: transcription start and termination, alternative splicing, RNA editing, polyadenylation, and the selection of coding and untranslated regions.

These processes generate distinct RNA isoforms from the same genomic locus. Each isoform is a linked molecular entity in which promoter choice, exon composition, coding sequence, editing events, and regulatory regions occur together on the same transcript. Preserving those relationships can be important because different isoforms may encode different proteins, differ in stability or localization, or respond differently to cellular regulation.

Conventional short-read RNA sequencing has transformed biology, but it typically fragments transcripts before sequencing. The resulting reads provide highly accurate local sequence information, while the full-length structures from which those reads originated must often be inferred computationally. Long-read sequencing can preserve more of the original transcript structure, but currently involves different trade-offs in instrumentation, throughput, depth, accuracy, cost, and workflow.

Phaeno’s PSeq technology is designed to preserve source-molecule identity through a short-read sequencing workflow. Reads derived from the same starting molecule can therefore be grouped before transcript assembly, converting an otherwise unconstrained reconstruction problem into a molecule-constrained one.

This article explains why that distinction matters. Part 2 will describe how PSeq preserves source-molecule identity, and Part 3 will examine the potential value of molecule-resolved transcript data in biological discovery, translational research, and machine learning.

## DNA sequence is not cellular phenotype

The Human Genome Project established a reference sequence for the human genome and provided an essential foundation for modern biology. It also helped clarify the limits of sequence information considered in isolation.

Cells with nearly identical genomes can adopt markedly different structures and functions. A hepatocyte, neuron, lymphocyte, and epithelial cell generally contain the same inherited DNA sequence, yet differ extensively in morphology, metabolism, signaling, and behavior. These differences arise from regulatory processes that determine which portions of the genome are used, how they are transcribed, and how the resulting RNAs are processed.

RNA occupies a central position in this process. It is both an intermediate between DNA and protein and, in many cases, a functional molecular species in its own right. Messenger RNAs encode proteins, while ribosomal, transfer, micro-, long noncoding, and other classes of RNA contribute directly to translation, regulation, genome organization, and cellular signaling.

Even within protein-coding genes, the relationship between a genomic locus and its biological products is not one-to-one. A single locus may produce multiple transcripts through combinations of:

- alternative transcription initiation;
- alternative splicing;
- alternative cleavage and polyadenylation;
- RNA editing;
- variation in untranslated regions;
- differential transcript stability and degradation.

The resulting transcripts may encode distinct protein sequences, alter the abundance of a protein, change where an RNA is localized, or affect its translation and degradation. The biological output of a gene therefore depends not only on whether the gene is expressed, but also on which transcripts are produced and in what proportions.

For this reason, RNA isoforms should not be treated merely as interchangeable measurements of gene activity. Each transcript is a linked arrangement of sequence and regulatory features that exists as a physical molecule.

## The transcript as a linked unit of information

A transcript may contain several biologically meaningful features simultaneously:

- its transcription start site;
- its exon and splice-junction structure;
- its open reading frame;
- sequence variants or RNA-editing events;
- its 5′ and 3′ untranslated regions;
- its cleavage and polyadenylation site.

The significance of any one feature may depend on the others with which it occurs.

A coding variant, for example, may be present only in transcripts initiated from a particular promoter. An exon-skipping event may alter the reading frame in one transcript context but not another. A 3′ untranslated region may contain regulatory motifs that affect the stability, localization, or translation of a specific coding isoform. Two transcripts from the same gene may therefore differ functionally even when they share most of their sequence.

This creates a phasing problem.

In sequencing, **phasing** means determining which sequence features occur together on the same physical molecule. The concept is familiar in genomics, where variants may be assigned to maternal or paternal chromosomes. In transcriptomics, the same principle applies to combinations of splice junctions, transcription boundaries, sequence variants, editing events, and regulatory regions.

Knowing that two features are present in a sample is not equivalent to knowing that they occur on the same transcript.

That distinction becomes especially important for genes that produce many overlapping isoforms. If several transcripts share exons and splice junctions, short sequence fragments may be compatible with multiple full-length structures. The local observations can be correct while the global arrangement remains ambiguous.

## The measurement problem created by fragmentation

Conventional RNA sequencing typically converts RNA into complementary DNA and fragments the material before or during library preparation. Millions of short reads are then aligned to a reference genome or transcriptome and summarized into gene-, exon-, junction-, or transcript-level measurements.

This approach has been extraordinarily productive. Short-read RNA sequencing supports:

- differential gene-expression analysis;
- splice-junction detection;
- transcript annotation;
- allele-specific expression;
- fusion detection;
- single-cell and spatial transcriptomics;
- numerous diagnostic and translational applications.

Its limitations should therefore be described precisely. The problem is not that short-read RNA sequencing fails to detect RNA or cannot identify transcript structure. Rather, fragmentation separates observations that originally occurred together on the same molecule.

Once source-molecule linkage has been lost, transcript reconstruction becomes an inference problem.

Computational methods can often resolve that problem, particularly when isoforms are well annotated, sequence coverage is high, and transcript structures are sufficiently distinct. Ambiguity increases, however, when a locus contains many overlapping isoforms that share exons, junctions, or sequence regions.

Consider a simplified gene containing two alternative promoters, several alternatively spliced exons, and two polyadenylation sites. Short reads may independently support both promoters, each splice junction, and both transcript ends. Yet those observations may remain compatible with several different full-length combinations.

The issue is therefore one of **identifiability**: whether the observed fragments uniquely determine the molecules that produced them.

For gene-level expression analysis, this ambiguity may be acceptable or irrelevant. For questions involving exact isoform structure, linked variants, RNA editing, or regulatory context, it may be consequential.

## Long-read sequencing addresses a different part of the problem

Long-read technologies from Oxford Nanopore Technologies and Pacific Biosciences approach transcript structure by generating reads that can span much or all of an RNA or complementary DNA molecule.

This is a major advantage for isoform discovery and transcript annotation. Long reads can directly connect distant splice junctions and transcript boundaries that short reads observe separately. They have expanded the number of detectable transcript structures and improved characterization of complex loci.

Long-read methods also have limitations and trade-offs. Depending on the platform, protocol, and experimental objective, these may include:

- the need for dedicated instrumentation;
- lower numbers of reads relative to high-throughput short-read systems;
- reduced sampling depth for low-abundance transcripts;
- differences in per-read accuracy and error profile;
- cost and workflow considerations;
- amplification or library-preparation biases;
- incomplete capture of transcript ends.

These technologies are improving rapidly, and no single description applies to every platform or application. Long-read and short-read methods should not be viewed as mutually exclusive. They answer overlapping but distinct questions and are increasingly used together.

The relevant question is not which platform is universally superior. It is which measurement architecture preserves the information required for a particular biological problem.

## PSeq: molecule-constrained assembly using short reads

PSeq is designed to retain source-molecule identity while using existing short-read next-generation sequencing instruments.

In the PSeq workflow, each reverse-transcribed source molecule is associated with molecular information that is retained as sequencing libraries are generated. After sequencing, reads carrying the same source-molecule identity can be grouped into molecule-specific sets.

Transcript assembly is then performed within those sets.

This changes the nature of the reconstruction problem. Conventional transcript assembly evaluates fragments drawn from a mixed population and identifies transcript models that can explain the aggregate data. PSeq instead seeks to assemble each transcript using reads assigned to the same starting molecule.

The assembly remains computational, but it is constrained by experimental information about molecular origin.

This distinction can be summarized as follows:

<ArticleCallout>
  <em>Conventional RNA sequencing asks which collection of transcripts most plausibly explains a mixed pool of reads. PSeq asks how reads known to originate from the same source molecule fit together.</em>
</ArticleCallout>

Because a source molecule may be represented by multiple independently sequenced fragments, consensus can be used to improve sequence accuracy. Actual performance will depend on factors including library complexity, molecule recovery, read depth, transcript length, sequence composition, and assembly algorithms. These characteristics must be established through analytical validation and comparative studies.

PSeq should therefore be understood as a new measurement architecture, not as a claim that every RNA molecule in a sample will necessarily be recovered completely or without error.

## RNA as a component of molecular phenotype

Phenotype is expressed across multiple biological scales. It includes observable traits, cellular states, tissue architecture, physiology, disease course, and response to treatment.

No single molecular layer is equivalent to phenotype. DNA, chromatin, RNA, protein, metabolites, cellular interactions, and environmental exposures all contribute.

The transcriptome nevertheless occupies a particularly informative position. It reflects both genomic potential and the current regulatory state of a biological system. It changes during differentiation, stress, immune activation, aging, treatment, and disease progression.

For that reason, the transcriptome can be considered one component of molecular phenotype: a dynamic record of which genomic programs are being executed and in what form.

Gene-expression measurements capture an important part of that record. Isoform-resolved measurements may capture another.

A useful shorthand is:

<ArticleCallout>
  <em>Gene-level expression indicates which loci are active. Isoform structure indicates which molecular messages those loci are producing.</em>
</ArticleCallout>

This distinction should not be overstated. Many biological questions are adequately addressed using gene-level counts, and increasing feature resolution can introduce additional noise, sparsity, and analytical complexity. The value of isoform-level data must therefore be demonstrated empirically for each application.

## Why molecule-resolved RNA may matter for machine learning

Machine-learning models do not automatically benefit from more detailed measurements. Additional features improve a model only when they contain reproducible information relevant to the prediction task and can be measured with sufficient consistency.

Isoform-resolved data could be useful because gene-level summaries may combine transcripts with different biological effects. If one isoform increases while another decreases, the total expression of the gene may remain unchanged even though the functional state of the system has shifted.

In such a case, a gene-level feature can obscure rather than reveal the relevant signal.

Molecule-resolved measurements could provide models with features such as:

- the abundance of defined full-length isoforms;
- linked combinations of splice and editing events;
- promoter-to-isoform relationships;
- coding sequences paired with untranslated regions;
- transcript structures not represented in existing annotations.

These features may be more closely related to molecular function than aggregate gene counts. They may also improve interpretability by allowing a model’s predictions to be associated with specific transcript structures.

There are, however, important challenges.

Isoform-level datasets are high-dimensional. Many isoforms are rare, tissue-specific, or incompletely annotated. Measurement error, batch effects, and changes in analysis pipelines can create unstable features. Larger numbers of candidate variables also increase the risk of overfitting.

The potential value of molecule-resolved RNA data for machine learning therefore depends on rigorous feature definition, analytical validation, independent cohorts, and prospective testing. It should be evaluated through direct comparisons against gene-level RNA sequencing and other established measurements.

The scientific hypothesis is not that isoform-level data will improve every model. It is that some biological signals are encoded in transcript structure and molecular linkage and are therefore inaccessible, diluted, or ambiguously represented in gene-level summaries.

## A different type of RNA measurement

The central distinction between conventional RNA sequencing and PSeq is not short reads versus long reads. PSeq also uses short reads.

The distinction is whether those reads retain information about their source molecule.

Conventional RNA sequencing fragments and pools transcripts, then infers full-length structures from aggregate evidence. PSeq is designed to fragment transcripts while retaining source-molecule identity, allowing each molecule to be reconstructed from its own associated reads.

Relevant benchmarks will include sensitivity, specificity, consensus accuracy, transcript completeness, isoform quantification, reproducibility, throughput, cost, and comparison with both short- and long-read methods.

Those studies are essential because molecule-resolved sequencing is useful only if molecular identity is preserved accurately and the resulting reconstructions reflect the molecules present in the original sample.

## Conclusion

RNA is not simply a transient copy of DNA. It is a diverse population of molecular species in which coding sequence, splice structure, transcription boundaries, regulatory regions, and sequence variation occur in linked combinations.

Conventional RNA sequencing measures this population with exceptional scale and accuracy, but fragmentation often separates features that originally belonged to the same molecule. Computational methods can reconstruct many transcript structures, yet ambiguity remains when multiple isoforms share the same local sequence evidence.

Long-read sequencing restores much of this connectivity by reading extended molecules directly. PSeq takes a different approach: preserving source-molecule identity through a short-read workflow and using that identity to constrain transcript assembly.

The potential significance of this approach is straightforward. If biological function depends on the complete structure of individual RNA molecules, then preserving those molecules as units of measurement may reveal information that is obscured by fragment-level or gene-level summaries.
