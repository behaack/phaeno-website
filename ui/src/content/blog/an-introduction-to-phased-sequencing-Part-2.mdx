## Summary

Phased sequencing depends on a simple idea: fragments are much more informative when their molecular origin remains known.

Conventional short-read RNA sequencing usually fragments and pools many transcripts before sequencing. The resulting reads can be accurate, abundant, and highly useful, but the experiment generally does not retain a direct record of which distant fragments came from the same starting RNA molecule. Full-length transcript structures must therefore be inferred from overlapping evidence collected across a mixed population.

PSeq is designed to change that measurement architecture. Molecular information is associated with each reverse-transcribed source molecule before short-fragment library generation. That information is intended to remain recoverable on the resulting reads, allowing reads from the same starting molecule to be grouped into molecule-specific sets before assembly.

The result is not a long read. It is a collection of short reads whose shared origin is known. Assembly can therefore be performed within a source-molecule boundary rather than across an undifferentiated pool of transcript fragments.

## What source-molecule identity means

Source-molecule identity is the experimental information that connects a sequencing observation to the RNA molecule from which it originated.

It is related to, but distinct from, ordinary sample indexing. A sample index identifies the specimen or library that produced a read. Source-molecule information operates at a finer level: it distinguishes individual starting molecules within that specimen or library.

It is also distinct from transcript annotation. An annotation may state that a read is compatible with a known gene or isoform. Source-molecule identity states that a particular set of reads originated from the same physical precursor, whether or not that precursor matches an existing annotation.

<ArticleCallout>
  <em>Sample identity answers which specimen a read came from. Source-molecule identity answers which molecule within that specimen a read came from.</em>
</ArticleCallout>

This distinction matters because two RNA molecules from the same gene may have different splice junctions, transcript boundaries, sequence variants, editing events, or untranslated regions. Once fragments from those molecules are mixed without a recoverable source relationship, their full-length arrangements become a computational inference problem.

## Why identity must be established before fragmentation

Fragmentation separates sequence features that were originally connected on the same molecule. A splice junction near the 5′ end, a coding variant in the middle, and a 3′ untranslated region may all be observed accurately, yet nothing in the fragment sequences alone necessarily proves that those features occurred together.

If molecular origin is established before fragmentation and carried into the sequencing library, the fragments can remain related even after the physical molecule has been divided. The connection is informational rather than spatial.

Failure at any stage can weaken the intended constraint. Collisions may make different molecules appear identical. Tag damage or sequencing error may split reads from one molecule into multiple groups. Contamination between groups may introduce fragments from unrelated molecules. These are measurable analytical risks, not merely software details.

## Identity constrains assembly; it does not solve it automatically

Source-molecule identity changes the search space available to an assembler. In conventional transcript assembly, many fragments from many transcripts may be compatible with the same gene. The algorithm must identify one or more transcript models that explain the pooled evidence.

In a molecule-constrained workflow, the assembler receives a smaller set of reads believed to originate from one starting molecule. The question changes from “Which population of transcripts explains these fragments?” to “How do these fragments fit together within this source molecule?”

That constraint can reduce ambiguity, but it does not eliminate every failure mode.

## Read assignment is an analytical measurement

It is tempting to treat molecular sorting as a preprocessing step. In practice, assignment quality is one of the measurements on which the downstream result depends.

Several error classes must be characterized:

- **Collisions:** two source molecules receive molecular information that is insufficiently distinct and are merged into one set.
- **Splitting:** reads from one source molecule appear to carry different identities and are divided across multiple sets.
- **Cross-assignment:** a fragment is placed in the wrong molecule-specific set.
- **Loss:** identity information cannot be read confidently, so otherwise useful sequence observations cannot be assigned.
- **Uneven representation:** some portions of a molecule are covered deeply while others are not recovered.

The acceptable rates depend on the application. Isoform discovery, abundance estimation, linked-variant analysis, and rare-molecule detection impose different requirements. A workflow intended for broad discovery may tolerate a different error profile than one intended to support a clinical decision.

## Consensus depends on independent evidence

Short-read sequencing can observe the same base position multiple times across fragments assigned to a molecule. When those observations are sufficiently independent and correctly assigned, agreement among them can support a consensus sequence.

Consensus is valuable because individual observations may contain sequencing or amplification errors. Repeated observations can help distinguish reproducible molecular sequence from sporadic error. But additional reads do not automatically produce an unbiased consensus. Shared errors introduced during reverse transcription, early copying, sequence-context effects, or systematic assignment mistakes may persist across the read set.

The relevant validation question is therefore not simply how many reads cover a molecule. It is whether the full workflow produces accurate, reproducible molecule-level sequences across realistic transcript lengths, abundances, compositions, and structures.

## What must be validated

A source-molecule workflow should be evaluated with reference materials whose expected sequences, structures, and abundances are known. Synthetic RNA controls, mixtures of defined isoforms, dilution series, replicate libraries, and orthogonal measurements can address different parts of the performance envelope.

Important measurements include:

- the number and fraction of source molecules recovered;
- the rate of molecular-identifier collision, splitting, loss, and cross-assignment;
- coverage uniformity across transcript bodies and ends;
- consensus accuracy by sequence context and depth;
- sensitivity and precision for known transcript structures;
- abundance linearity and reproducibility across replicates;
- performance across transcript length, abundance, GC content, and structural complexity;
- robustness to sample quality, input amount, batch, operator, and instrument variation.

These measurements should be reported separately. A high consensus accuracy among successfully reconstructed molecules does not establish high molecule recovery. Accurate transcript structures do not by themselves establish unbiased quantification. Strong performance on synthetic controls does not guarantee equivalent performance in complex biological samples.

## Scientific context for molecular identifiers

The general principle of molecule-aware sequencing predates PSeq. Kivioja and colleagues demonstrated that [unique molecular identifiers can distinguish individual nucleic-acid molecules](https://doi.org/10.1038/nmeth.1778) and support molecule-aware counting in next-generation sequencing.

PSeq applies source-molecule information toward a different objective: constraining the reconstruction of full transcript molecules from short-fragment reads. The scientific precedent supports the value of molecular identification as a measurement principle; it does not substitute for validation of PSeq chemistry, assignment, assembly, or consensus performance.

## Conclusion

PSeq is designed to preserve the relationship between a source molecule and the short reads derived from it. Molecular information is associated before fragmentation, carried through library generation, recovered during sequencing, and used to create molecule-specific read sets.

That relationship changes transcript assembly from a population-level inference problem into a molecule-constrained reconstruction problem. It may also provide repeated sequence observations that support consensus generation.

The value of the architecture depends on the integrity of the entire chain. Molecular information must be sufficiently diverse, stable, readable, and error-tolerant. Read assignment must be accurate. Coverage must support the intended reconstruction. Consensus and quantification must be validated independently.
